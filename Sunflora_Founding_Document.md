# Sunflora — Founding Document

*A handmade, personalized gifting brand. Built from the founder's own answers, not generic startup advice. Honest about what's proven, what's a guess, and what to do next.*

**Instagram:** @sunflora_official
**Status at time of writing:** Pre-launch. 2 finished sample pieces made (a bouquet + a decorative mala). Hero product (the personalized photo frame) not yet built. Zero sales.

---

## 0. The one-line version

Sunflora turns a customer's own photo into a handmade 3D flower frame — a personal memory made physical, that never fades. Sold mainly to young people (18–30) as a romantic or heartfelt gift, starting from the founder's own network of ~100 warm contacts.

---

## 1. Origin & Motivation

The founder makes handmade items from pipe cleaner (fuzzy craft wire) — flower bouquets, flower mala for God/puja, customized 3D flower frames, and dream catchers. The instinct was to build a simple website to showcase these products systematically with photos.

The honest starting point: this began as a making/craft skill looking for a business around it. That's a fine place to start — the founder can actually make the product with their own hands, which is more than most "ideas" ever have. But the conversation that produced this document deliberately pushed past "I want a website" to answer the harder questions first: *who buys this, why, and why from Sunflora.*

---

## 2. The Problem / Desire Being Served

People want to give gifts that feel personal and meaningful — not generic. Real flowers die in two days. A shop-bought frame has no personal story. Sunflora sells the thing in between: a personal object, made by hand, that lasts.

**Honest evidence status:** This is currently a *hypothesis, not a proven fact.* No stranger has paid for it yet. The single most important job ahead is not the website or the branding — it is getting the first stranger to hand over money. Until that happens, everything else is decoration on an unproven idea. This is stated plainly here so it is never forgotten.

---

## 3. Target Customer (v1)

**Primary customer:** Young people, roughly **18–30**, buying a **romantic or friendship gift** — for a boyfriend/girlfriend, best friend, or "I saw this and thought of you" moment. Instagram-native. They value the gift looking cute and feeling personal, and they will post and tag it when they love it.

**Why this customer was chosen over the alternatives:** They double as free marketing. When a 23-year-old receives a custom photo-flower frame with an inside joke on it, she posts it, tags Sunflora, and her whole friend group sees it. That reach cannot be bought — and the older/family gift buyer does not spread the same way.

**The two real downsides of this customer (eyes open):**
1. **Price-sensitive.** Many won't easily pay ₹1,000 for a gift. This shapes pricing directly (see §7).
2. **Seasonal.** They buy in bursts — Valentine's, Friendship Day, birthday-heavy months — with quiet stretches between.

**Explicitly rejected framing:** "Everyone likes a gift / 16–60 / anyone." This was raised more than once and pushed back on each time. "Everyone" is not a customer — it produces a website that speaks to no one and marketing that reaches no one. Narrowing the *target* does not lock the door on other buyers (an aunty can still buy a frame aimed at young couples); it just lights a path so people can find the brand.

---

## 4. The Product & MVP Scope

**The real MVP is not the website.** It is *proof that a stranger will pay.* The website is a tool that comes later, once there is proof to display.

**Product ladder (good-better-best):**

| Product | Role | Time to make | Rough price |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Mini frame** | Entry / "try me" for nervous first-time & young buyers | Must be **under ~45 min** | ~₹400–500 |
| **Signature frame** (customer's photo + 3D flowers) | **The hero.** Special-occasion, premium | ~2 hrs | ~₹900–1,200 |
| **Bouquet** | Add-on / cheaper alternative | — | Lower ticket |

**Critical rule on the mini frame:** It must take *less of the founder's time*, not just cost less in materials. If it still takes 2 hours, it just earns less money for the same work — which is worse, not better. Under ~45 minutes or it fails its purpose.

**What's IN v1:** The signature photo frame (hero) + mini frame + bouquet as add-on.
**Deliberately cut / de-prioritized for now:** Mala, dream catchers, and chasing multiple customer types at once. These can return later; they are not the wedge.

**Immediate build gap:** The hero product — the personalized photo frame — *has not been made yet.* Only the bouquet and mala exist. First physical task before any website: **make 3–4 sample photo frames** to show and photograph.

---

## 5. Unique Selling Proposition — Why Sunflora

The USP is **NOT "good quality"** and **NOT "less competition in my area."** Both were raised and rejected, for concrete reasons:

- *"Quality"* is the founder's own opinion of their own work (every maker believes this), can't be proven in a photo, and can't be marketed to a stranger before they buy.
- *"Less competition near me"* is irrelevant — the customer is on Instagram and orders ship by courier, so the real competition is **every craft seller in India who can post a parcel,** not the neighbourhood. The "area" is the internet.

**The actual USP — a stacked pitch:**

> **"Turn your favourite photo into a handmade flower frame that never fades — a memory you can keep forever."**

- **Memory / one-of-one (the hook that's hard to copy):** A frame built around *the customer's specific photo* cannot exist for anyone else. Nobody on Amazon or at a florist has *that* photo with *those* flowers. This is the genuine edge.
- **"Lasts forever" (the closer that's easy to believe):** Real flowers die; these never wilt. This reassures and justifies the price — but on its own it's weak (every artificial-flower seller says it), so it *supports* the memory angle rather than leading.

"Memory made physical" is a feature of Sunflora. "Never wilts" is a feature of the material. Lead with the former.

---

## 6. Competitive Landscape

- **Direct:** Thousands of handmade-gift and pipe-cleaner sellers across India on Instagram, many with polished pages, followings, and years of reviews. This is the real arena.
- **Indirect:** Florists (fresh flowers), personalized-gift brands, and generic gift shops / Amazon.

**How Sunflora survives against bigger players:** Not on price, not on volume — it *can't* win those (see §9 capacity). It wins on **hyper-personalization + meaning**: the one-of-one photo frame that a factory or high-volume seller doesn't bother to make. Capacity limits *force* the brand upmarket, and that aligns perfectly with the premium personalized hero product.

---

## 7. Business Model & Pricing Logic

**The core tension of the whole business, stated honestly:**
> The hero product takes real time and can't be cheap — but the chosen customer is the one *least* able to pay premium prices.

This is managed with the product ladder (cheap mini frame to lower the risk of a first purchase; premium signature frame for real money), not ignored.

**The pricing math (done properly, including the founder's own time):**

- Signature frame: **2 hours** of labour + **₹200–300** materials.
- Time is a real cost. At even a modest ₹150/hr, 2 hrs = ₹300 labour.
- ₹300 labour + ₹300 materials = **~₹600 just to break even** — before packaging, delivery, any ad money, and before *profit*.
- Therefore a real selling price is **~₹900–1,200** for the signature frame to actually make money.

**Ironclad payment rule:** **Money first, then it gets made.** Every piece is custom (built around a stranger's photo), so an unpaid or refused custom frame can *never* be resold. Upfront payment is not negotiable.

## 8. Go-To-Market — The First 10, Then the First 100

**The most important section in this document.** A business is simply "can you repeatedly get strangers to pay you." Everything else is set dressing.

**The trap to avoid:** "Post on Instagram and wait for orders." With ~5 followers, posts reach almost no one. Posting and waiting is a prayer, not a plan.

**How the first customers actually come — the engine:**

1. **Warm circle first.** The founder has ~**100 reachable people** across family/cousins, friends, WhatsApp groups, and — importantly — **parents/students from their DSE education-guidance work**, who already know and trust their name. This is a real, existing network and a genuine asset.
2. **Realistic first-round conversion:** out of ~100 warm contacts, expect **5–10 to actually buy** at launch (not 100 — anyone promising that is lying). That's *enough*: it's the proof, the first reviews, and the first real buyer photos.
3. **Their circles become customers 11–50.** Each happy buyer posts/shows friends; those friends are the next wave.
4. **Targeted DMs beat broadcast posts.** Messaging a specific person with a specific reason ("your anniversary's coming — want me to make something?") beats shouting into an empty feed.
5. **Local micro-events later:** a college fest stall, a society festival market, being "the gift everyone asked about" at a friend's birthday.

**None of this requires a website.** It requires the founder, WhatsApp, good photos of 2–3 real frames, and the nerve to ask.

---

## 9. Production / Delivery — Capacity & What Breaks First

**Weekly ceiling:** The founder can realistically make **5–10 frames/week** alongside their guidance work and normal life.

**What this means (most makers never do this math):** At 5–10/week × ~₹900–1,200, the *absolute* monthly revenue ceiling is roughly **₹20,000–40,000/month** if every piece sells. This is a solid side-income — and it confirms the strategy: **never compete on cheap or high-volume.** Sunflora physically cannot out-produce a factory. Fewer pieces, higher price, more meaning is the only path.

**What breaks first in a rush:** *Time.* If a festival week brings 15 orders and capacity is 10, the founder must either disappoint people or deliver late — and a gift that arrives *after* the occasion is worthless. **Fix: caps and cutoffs** — e.g. "only 8 slots this week," "order 5 days before your date." Scarcity also makes the brand look more premium.

**Delivery:** Courier via **Delhivery**. 
- *Watch-out:* frames are bulky/fragile — shipping may run ₹80–150+. Decide: add shipping on top, or bake "free shipping" into a higher price (free *feels* better to buyers even when baked in). **Open decision.**
- *Non-negotiable:* sturdy, protective packaging (bubble wrap + strong box). A cracked frame = a furious buyer + a public bad review, which for a tiny new brand is fatal.

**Payment:** **Online only, NO COD.** This is the correct call — COD on a custom, unresellable, made-to-order item is dangerous (refused parcel = a frame with a stranger's face on it that can never be resold, plus two-way courier cost). Start with **UPI/GPay** — free, no payment gateway needed yet.

## 10. Branding & Positioning

- **Name:** **Sunflora** — warm, brandable (not just descriptive), pronounceable, memorable. Signals "flora/flowers" softly without locking the brand into flowers-only. Good choice.
- **Handle:** @sunflora_official (live and secured). *Note for later:* "_official" reads like a big brand guarding against fakes; a cleaner @sunflora / @sunflora.in feels warmer and more trustworthy for a small brand. Not worth changing today — noted only.
- **Tagline:** "Craftilicious" is **retired.** It reads playful/food-like and says "craft" — describing the *hobby*, not the *feeling the customer buys*. Replace with something that sells memory/forever, e.g. *"memories that never fade"* or *"a memory you can keep forever."*
- **Tone/identity:** Warm, personal, heartfelt — the opposite of "generic craft stall." Packaging is part of the brand: a gift arriving in a beautiful box gets photographed and posted, which for a gifting brand *is* marketing.

---

## 11. Marketing Channels & Assumptions

- **Primary:** Instagram + WhatsApp, powered by *real buyer photos and reviews* — not an empty polished page.
- **Trust comes from proof, not from a website.** A nice website with 5 followers and zero reviews makes strangers *more* suspicious, not less. Order: get buyers → collect reviews/photos → *then* the website/ads have something real to show.
- **Paid Instagram boost: NOT yet.** Boosting sends an empty, review-less shop to strangers who have no reason to trust it — money burned. Only after ~10 sales + real reviews. Until then, that ₹500–1,000 goes to **better packaging** instead (free reach when buyers post their gift).
- **Best free reach:** post-worthy pieces + beautiful packaging that buyers share themselves.

---

## 12. Team & Skill Gaps

- **Team:** Solo founder — maker, seller, packer, shipper, marketer, all in one. Also runs DSE education-guidance work in parallel (a time constraint *and* a warm-network asset).
- **Skill/comfort gap (the honest one):** The founder is strong and comfortable on the *making and planning* side, and less comfortable on the *asking-a-stranger-to-pay* side (see §15). This, not craft skill, is the real gap to close.
- **Not a strong coder** — another reason to delay the website and lean on WhatsApp/Instagram first.

---

## 13. Legal / Regulatory / Practical

- **Customer photos:** The business is built on customers sending personal photos (of partners, mothers, friends). Handle these respectfully — use them only to make the ordered piece; get simple permission before ever reposting a customer's personal photo publicly. **Open task: a one-line "we only use your photo for your order" note builds trust.**
- **Payments:** UPI/GPay to start is fine and legal for a small operation. Formal registration (e.g. GST) is not needed at this tiny scale but should be revisited as revenue grows.
- **No COD** already protects against the biggest practical/financial risk on custom goods.
- **IP:** The personal, one-of-one nature of each frame is itself hard to copy — the real "moat" is the customer relationship and the buyer's own photo, not a patent.

## 14. Financials — Startup Cost & Runway

**Realistic cost to start properly: ~₹5,000–8,000.**

| Item | Rough cost |
|---|---|
| Bulk pipe cleaners, wire, glue, supplies (~10–15 pieces) | ₹1,500–2,500 |
| Blank frames + photo printing (samples + first orders) | ₹1,000–2,000 |
| Packaging (boxes, wrap, ribbon — matters a lot for a gift) | ₹1,000–1,500 |
| Small optional Instagram boost — **defer until after 10 sales** | ₹500–1,000 (later) |

**Key strength:** This is a genuinely low-cost, low-risk business to start. No lakhs at stake — roughly the cost of a phone bill to test whether strangers will pay. **No website cost, no logo designer, no equipment needed to begin** — all optional and later.

*(Founder to confirm this amount is comfortably available; if not, the start can be scaled down further — even 3–4 samples' worth of materials is enough to begin testing.)*

## 15. Success Metrics & Milestones

**90-day scoreboard (in priority order):**

1. **10 paying customers** ← the real goal. Paying customers = proof + reviews + photos + word-of-mouth. Everything flows from this.
2. **~₹15,000 revenue** ← follows naturally from the 10 customers; a health-check that pricing isn't too low.
3. **5–8 real reviews / buyer photos** ← the trust assets that make the *next* stranger buy.

**Vanity-metric warning:** *Followers are a byproduct, not a target.* 50 followers who never buy is worthless; 10 buyers with 5 followers is a business. Track followers loosely; never let "get more followers" pull focus from "get more buyers." Buyers bring followers, not the other way around.

**Sequenced next actions (the correct order):**
1. Make 3–4 sample photo frames.
2. Shoot clean phone photos (natural light, plain background).
3. Message the ~100 warm contacts with a simple, specific offer.
4. Land the first 5–10 orders; collect finished-piece + happy-buyer photos and their words.
5. **Only then** build the website — now it has products, prices, real photos, and reviews to convert with. (Website is needed around order #20, not order #0.)

---

## 16. Biggest Risk & How to De-Risk It

**The #1 risk is not the product, price, or competition. It is the gap between planning and asking.**

Throughout the founding process, the comfortable tasks (name, website, ads) were reached for repeatedly, while the uncomfortable one — *making samples and asking 100 real people "will you pay me ₹1,000 for this?"* — was steered away from. That is deeply human and extremely common. But the entire business is downstream of that one scary act. Every plan in this document is real and good — and none of it matters if the founder builds and plans forever but freezes at the moment of asking people to pay.

**The de-risk is brutally simple:**
> **Do not touch the website until at least 20 real people have been asked to buy.** Force the scary thing first.

**Secondary risks & mitigations:**
- *Price resistance from young buyers* → the mini frame lowers the entry risk; lead with meaning, not price.
- *Seasonal demand + capacity crunch* → order cutoffs and weekly slot caps; push hardest in gifting seasons.
- *Fragile-delivery breakage / bad reviews* → serious protective packaging, non-negotiable.
- *Time split with guidance work* → keep the weekly cap realistic (5–10); don't over-promise.

---

## 17. Honest Summary

Sunflora is a **real, low-cost, low-risk business worth testing** — with a genuine edge (one-of-one personalized photo frames), a real starting network (~100 warm contacts including trusting education-work parents), and honest pricing math already done.

What it is **not yet**: proven. Zero strangers have paid. The hero product isn't built. The whole thing now hinges on doing the uncomfortable, un-glamorous work in order: **make samples → photograph → ask your people → get the first 10 → then, and only then, build the shop.**

The plan is sound. The only question left is whether the founder will do the asking. Everything in this document is designed to get to that one moment.

## 18. Website — Build & Audit Status (updated 2026-07-23)

*This section is a factual status update appended after the original document. The strategy above (§0–§17) still stands; this records where the actual website now is.*

**The website exists and is live.** It is built with Next.js 15 (App Router) + React 19, TypeScript, hosted on Vercel, and lives at **www.sunflora.shop** (the founder's owned domain; the audit wired the code to it after finding it had been pointing at a dead placeholder domain). All content and product data live in one file, `web/lib/site.ts`. There is no cart or checkout — every "DM to order" button opens an Instagram DM, matching the go-to-market plan in §8 and §11.

**Products currently on the site:** Signature Frame (₹900–1,200), Mini Frame (₹400–500), Everlasting Bouquet (two colourways, price TBD), Lotus Latkan and Purple Lotus Latkan (₹800/pair). This is broader than the §4 v1 scope (which deliberately cut the mala/latkan) — a note for future focus, not a correction.

**A full professional audit was completed (2026-07-23)** across engineering, conversion design and marketing. Sixteen fixes were made on a branch (`audit/repairs`), including: repairing the dead-domain problem that was breaking every shared link's preview image; cutting the mobile image payload from ~3.4 MB to ~1 MB; fixing a mobile "See details" button that was silently rendering as a tiny text link; removing duplicate gallery photos; adding a branded 404 page; an always-visible mobile "DM to order" bar; accessibility and contrast fixes; a Content-Security-Policy header; and search/share metadata (structured data, correct titles, sitemap). Full detail lives in the audit deliverables (`audit/` — kept local) and the private change log.

**Open items awaiting founder decision (as of this update):**

**The §16 warning still governs.** The website is now good — arguably ahead of the business. Per this document's own logic, the highest-return next action is still *not* on the website: it is messaging the ~100 warm contacts and landing the first 10 paid orders. The site is now a link worth putting under that message; its job is to close warm doubt, not manufacture cold demand.
